High Concern — Act Quickly If You See These

Pests That Need Fast Attention in the Philly Area

These are the issues most likely to drive a late-night search, an urgent call, or a commercial complaint. They reproduce quickly, spread between units, or cause structural damage.

If you are seeing repeat activity, finding evidence beyond a single bug, or managing a commercial property — treat these as high priority and submit a request with photos and details.

High Concern

Bed Bugs

Common searches: "bug in bed," "bed bug shells," "bites after sleeping," "tiny brown bug on mattress."

  • What to look for: Apple-seed-sized, reddish-brown, flat. Found in mattress seams, box springs, bed frame joints, headboards, outlet covers, and furniture near sleeping areas.
  • Evidence: Dark reddish-brown spotting or smearing on fabric, shed skins (translucent husks), eggs (tiny white), and a sweet musty odor in heavy infestations.
  • Where in Philadelphia region: Multi-unit buildings, hotels, rental properties, and any dwelling with frequent overnight visitors. Not linked to cleanliness.
  • Common confusion: Carpet beetles and their larvae, bat bugs, booklice, ticks, and small roach nymphs.
  • What to do: Do not throw furniture out immediately. Document with photos, note where bites occur on the body, and submit a request with the building type and number of units.
Spreads between unitsBedroom & furnitureMulti-unit risk
High Concern

German Cockroaches

Common searches: "small roach in kitchen," "baby roach," "roach in restaurant," "roach eggs under sink."

  • What to look for: Small (½–⅝ inch), tan to light brown with two dark parallel stripes behind the head. Nymphs are darker and smaller.
  • Evidence: Black pepper-like droppings near appliances, behind refrigerators, under sinks, and in cabinet hinges. Egg cases (ootheca) attached to surfaces. Musty odor. Activity when lights come on.
  • Where in Philadelphia region: Kitchens, bathrooms, restaurants, food-service spaces, break rooms, apartment buildings. Common in row houses and multi-unit properties throughout Philadelphia, Camden, and Wilmington.
  • Common confusion: Wood roaches (less concerning, outdoor invader), oriental roaches (larger, darker), beetles.
  • What to do: Fast response matters — German roaches reproduce rapidly. Document droppings location, property type, and whether it is a rental or commercial space.

High Concern

Termites (Eastern Subterranean)

Common searches: "termite swarmers," "wings on windowsill," "mud tubes in basement," "termite or flying ant."

  • What to look for: Spring swarmers emerge from soil or wood near foundations. Creamy white workers are rarely seen without disturbing infested wood. Mud tubes (pencil-width dirt tunnels) on foundation walls, in crawl spaces, and along joists are a key sign.
  • Evidence: Discarded wings near windows and doors in spring, hollow-sounding wood when tapped, buckling paint or flooring, and mud tubes at the foundation.
  • Where in Philadelphia region: Eastern subterranean termites are the dominant species in PA, NJ, and DE. Properties near wooded areas, older homes, and crawl spaces are especially common targets.
  • Common confusion: Flying ants (different wing shape and waist — save a sample), drain flies, and other spring-emerging insects.
  • What to do: Do not disturb mud tubes before an inspection — they are evidence. Document with photos and note the location in the structure.
Structural damageSpring swarmsInspection needed
High Concern

Bed Bug Look-alikes: What Triggers False Alarms

Common searches: "is this a bed bug?" "tiny bug in bed," "brown bug on pillow."

  • Carpet beetles: Oval, mottled brown or varied pattern. Often found near fabric, windowsills, stored items, closets. Larvae look like small, hairy worms. Not a blood feeder — but can cause skin irritation from bristle hairs.
  • Bat bugs: Nearly identical to bed bugs visually. Key clue: presence of bats roosting in attic, walls, or chimney. The bat bug's fringe hairs are slightly longer under magnification.
  • Booklice (Psocids): Tiny, pale, and soft-bodied. Often found in humid areas near paper, books, or stored food. Attracted to mold, not blood. Common in basements and older buildings.
  • Ticks: Eight legs (arachnid, not insect). Flat when unfed, swell after a blood meal. Most often brought in on pets or after outdoor activity. Require different treatment path than bed bugs.

Urgent in Commercial Spaces

American & Oriental Cockroaches

Common searches: "large roach in basement," "water bug," "big black roach in kitchen."

  • American roach: Large (1.5–2 inches), reddish-brown, can fly. Often called "water bugs." Found in basements, sewers, steam tunnels, and utility areas. Common in older urban buildings throughout Philadelphia.
  • Oriental roach: Dark brown to black, 1 inch, sluggish. Found in basements, drains, damp crawl spaces, and exterior debris piles near buildings.
  • What to look for: Large droppings (blunt-ended), musty odor, activity near floor drains and utility openings. Often a sign of moisture or drainage issues, not just pest pressure.
  • Common confusion: German roaches (much smaller), wood roaches (outdoor invader), ground beetles.
Moisture indicatorBasements & drainsUrban buildings
Needs Inspection

Carpenter Ants

Common searches: "large black ant in kitchen," "carpenter ants in walls," "sawdust near window frame."

  • What to look for: Large (¼–½ inch), black or black-and-red, smooth thorax. Most active at night. Winged reproductive forms can be confused with termite swarmers in spring.
  • Evidence: Coarse sawdust-like frass (wood shavings mixed with insulation and dead insect parts) pushed out of walls or near wooden structures. Rustling sounds in walls at night. Activity near moisture-damaged wood, window frames, decks, and roof eaves.
  • Where in Philadelphia region: Common in older homes, wooded suburbs in Montgomery, Bucks, Chester, and Camden counties. Often a secondary sign of moisture damage or wood rot.
  • Common confusion: Termite swarmers (different wing and waist shape), pavement ants, other large ant species.

Rodents — Signs, Species, and What They Mean

Mice and Rats in the Greater Philadelphia Area

Rodent activity tends to increase in fall when temperatures drop and again in spring. Philadelphia's row houses, older buildings, and active restaurant corridors make rodents a consistent commercial and residential concern.

The difference between mice and rats matters for treatment planning. Norway rats and house mice behave differently and require different approaches.

House Mouse

Common searches: "mouse droppings," "scratching in walls at night," "mouse in kitchen," "small black droppings near stove."

  • Small (2.5–4 inches body), gray-brown with large ears and a pointed snout. Tail is as long as the body.
  • Droppings are small (3–6 mm), dark, and rod-shaped with pointed ends.
  • Entry points as small as a dime (¼ inch). Common entry via gaps around pipes, utility lines, dryer vents, and foundation cracks.
  • Signs: gnaw marks on food packaging, shredded nesting material (insulation, paper), grease smudges along walls, and scratching at night.
  • Common in Philadelphia row homes, apartments, restaurants, and commercial kitchens.
Interior nestingFood contamination

Norway Rat (Brown Rat)

Common searches: "large rat in yard," "rat droppings near dumpster," "rat hole in garden," "rat in restaurant basement."

  • Large (7–10 inches body), brown-gray, heavy-bodied with a blunt nose and small ears. Tail is shorter than the body.
  • Droppings are large (12–20 mm), dark, and blunt-ended.
  • Burrows in soil near foundations, compost piles, and beneath decking. Also nests in wall voids of commercial buildings.
  • Signs: burrow holes (2–3 inch diameter), large greasy rub marks along foundation walls, gnaw damage on wiring and structural materials.
  • Particularly common in alleys, behind restaurants, near loading docks, and in dense commercial areas of Philadelphia, Camden, and Wilmington.
Commercial concernExterior burrowsSanitation risk

Deer Mouse

Less common in urban cores, more common in suburban and semi-rural areas of Bucks County, Chester County, and New Jersey's Burlington and Gloucester counties.

  • Bi-colored: brown body with white underside, white feet, and a bi-colored tail.
  • Often found in garages, sheds, storage areas, and properties adjacent to fields or wooded edges.
  • Hantavirus association: deer mice can carry hantavirus. Use caution when cleaning droppings or nesting material in enclosed spaces — do not dry-sweep.
  • Common confusion: house mice (solid gray-brown, no two-tone pattern).
Suburban & rural areasGarages & sheds

Stinging Insects — Identification and Urgency

Wasps, Yellow Jackets & Hornets

Stinging insects are one of the most common urgent requests for the Greater Philadelphia area from late spring through early fall. Nest location and size determine the risk level.

Nests near entryways, under eaves, and in wall voids are the highest priority. Nests in trees away from foot traffic are often lower urgency unless disturbed.

Yellow Jackets

Common searches: "yellow jacket nest in ground," "wasps coming from wall," "yellow jackets near deck."

  • Bright yellow and black, smooth-bodied, about ½ inch. Extremely aggressive when nests are disturbed.
  • Nest in soil (look for a hole with heavy traffic), wall voids, and hollow structures. Paper nests in ground are common from July–October.
  • Peak aggression in late summer and fall when the colony is largest and food sources are scarce.
  • Common confusion: honeybees (rounder, fuzzy, less aggressive, protected), paper wasps (longer legs, slender).
Sting riskLate summer peak

Bald-faced Hornets

Common searches: "large papery nest in tree," "black and white wasp," "hornet nest under eave."

  • Large (¾ inch), black with white facial markings. Build large gray paper nests in trees, shrubs, under eaves, and on outdoor structures.
  • Nests start golf-ball size in spring and grow to basketball size or larger by late summer.
  • Very aggressive defenders — approach or vibration near the nest triggers a rapid defense response.
  • Nests are abandoned in winter but the structure remains. Do not treat an abandoned nest — wait until spring to confirm it is empty before removing it.
Aggressive defenseVisible paper nest

Paper Wasps

Common searches: "small wasp nest under deck," "wasp nest on porch ceiling," "papery nest on eave."

  • Brown with yellow markings, long dangling legs in flight. Build open umbrella-shaped paper nests with visible cells.
  • Nests under decks, porch ceilings, doorframes, eave overhangs, and behind shutters. Generally less aggressive than yellow jackets unless the nest is touched.
  • Beneficial predators of garden caterpillars and flies — but nests near entryways or high-traffic areas warrant treatment.
Eaves & doorframesOpen-cell nest

Seasonal Invaders & Exterior Pests

Pests That Follow the Seasons in PA, NJ, and DE

Many of the most searched pests in the Philly region are not building infestations — they are seasonal migrants entering structures from outside as temperatures shift.

The distinction matters. An exterior invader needs a different plan than an indoor-reproducing pest. Sending a photo and location helps narrow this quickly.

Brown Marmorated Stink Bug

Common searches: "stink bug in house," "why are there stink bugs on my windows," "stink bugs in fall."

  • Shield-shaped, mottled brown and gray, about ¾ inch. Emits a cilantro-like odor when crushed or disturbed.
  • Congregates on south- and west-facing walls in fall, enters homes through gaps around windows, doors, utility penetrations, and siding.
  • Overwintering pest — does not reproduce indoors and does not cause structural damage.
  • Most common in suburbs of PA, NJ, and DE near orchards, trees, and ornamental plantings.
Fall invaderDoes not reproduce indoors

Boxelder Bugs

Common searches: "red and black bugs on house," "bugs near maple tree," "black bugs clustering on windows."

  • Flat, black with orange-red markings and wing veins, about ½ inch. Often found in large clusters on sunny walls in fall.
  • Associated with boxelder trees (a type of maple), silver maples, and ash trees near the property.
  • Enter homes in fall to overwinter, emerge in spring. No structural damage, no indoor reproduction.
  • Common confusion: milkweed bugs (similar color, found on milkweed), squash bugs (garden pest).
Fall invaderNear maple & boxelder trees

Ticks

Common searches: "tick bite," "deer tick found on dog," "tick in yard," "what kind of tick is this."

  • Black-legged (Deer) Tick: Tiny (sesame seed to apple seed), reddish-brown body with black legs. Primary vector of Lyme disease in the Northeast. Active in PA, NJ, and DE year-round above 35°F.
  • American Dog Tick: Larger, brown with white-gray markings. Active spring through early fall. Less of a Lyme concern but can transmit Rocky Mountain spotted fever.
  • Found in leaf litter, tall grass, shrub edges, and wood piles. Most tick exposure happens at transition zones between lawn and wooded areas.
  • Not a pest control service in the traditional sense — but tick pressure in yard environments is manageable. Note property size and proximity to wooded edges.
Health concernYard environmentsPet & family risk

Fleas

Common searches: "tiny jumping bugs," "flea bites on ankles," "small bugs on dog bed," "bugs in carpet after vacation."

  • Tiny (1/16 inch), flat, dark brown, and nearly invisible without close inspection. Identified by jumping behavior and association with pets or wildlife.
  • Bites commonly appear on ankles, lower legs, and around waistbands. Random pattern, not linear like bed bug bites.
  • Egg and larval stages hide in carpet fibers, bedding, pet resting areas, and furniture upholstery. Adult fleas on the pet are only 5% of the population — 95% is in the environment.
  • Common confusion: springtails (jump but found near moisture and don't bite), carpet beetles (no jumping), gnats.
Pet-conscious treatmentCarpet & bedding

Centipedes (House Centipede)

Common searches: "fast bug with lots of legs," "centipede in bathroom," "spider-looking bug in shower."

  • Long, flat, yellowish-gray with 15 pairs of long legs and long antennae. Very fast-moving. Startling in appearance but not aggressive toward people.
  • Found in basements, bathrooms, and damp areas. An indicator of moisture and the presence of other small insects they prey on.
  • Not a destructive pest — but a persistent presence usually signals a moisture issue or an underlying prey insect population worth investigating.
  • Common confusion: millipedes (slower, rounded, more legs, does not bite), earwigs (smaller, pincer at rear).
Moisture indicatorBasements & bathrooms

Silverfish

Common searches: "silver bug in bathroom," "bug in book," "fish-shaped insect in closet," "silverfish in basement."

  • Teardrop-shaped, silver to gray, ½–¾ inch, with three long tail bristles. Moves in a fish-like wriggling motion. Fast-moving but not aggressive.
  • Attracted to humidity, starchy materials, paper, adhesive, and protein sources. Common in bathrooms, basements, attics, and old book storage.
  • Signs: irregular feeding damage on wallpaper, book pages, fabric, and stored paper products. Yellow staining near feeding areas.
  • Not a dangerous pest, but persistent populations in libraries, historical properties, and archives can cause real damage to materials.
Humidity-drivenPaper & fabric damage

Kitchen, Food & Moisture Pests

Insects Triggered by Food, Moisture, and Organic Material

These pests thrive in kitchens, drain lines, pantries, restaurant prep areas, and any space with food residue or standing moisture. They are common complaints for both homeowners and food-service businesses.

Drain flies and fruit flies in a restaurant or bar setting are a common health inspection trigger. Fast identification helps prioritize the source.

Fruit Flies & Drain Flies

  • Fruit flies (Drosophila): Small (⅛ inch), tan-brown with red eyes. Attracted to ripening fruit, sugary spills, fermenting organic matter, and bar drains. Can complete a lifecycle in 8–10 days at room temperature.
  • Drain flies (moth flies): Tiny, fuzzy, moth-like appearance. Found resting on walls near drains, sinks, and anywhere organic material builds up inside pipes. Common in bar floor drains, bathroom floor drains, and commercial kitchen floor sinks.
  • Key distinction: fruit flies hover and fly when disturbed; drain flies tend to rest on surfaces and are weak fliers.
  • Commercial concern: both can trigger health code violations in food-service settings.
Health code concernBars & restaurants

Pantry Pests (Stored Product Insects)

  • Indian meal moth: Small moth with copper-brown outer wings. Larvae found in flour, cereals, dried fruit, pet food, and grains. Webbing inside packaging is a key sign.
  • Grain weevils: Small, dark brown, with a snout. Found in intact whole grains, rice, corn, and stored seeds. Common in pantries and restaurant dry storage.
  • Saw-toothed grain beetle: Tiny, flat, with saw-like serrations on the thorax. Found in flour, pasta, cereals, and processed dry goods.
  • First step: check all dry goods, including sealed boxes — many stored product insects can penetrate thin cardboard packaging.
Dry storageRestaurant pantries

Ants: Odorous House Ants & Pavement Ants

Common searches: "tiny ants in kitchen," "ant trail on counter," "black ants near window."

  • Odorous house ants: Tiny (⅛ inch), dark brown to black, emit a coconut-like smell when crushed. Highly organized trails. Nest indoors near moisture — under sinks, in wall voids, beneath floors.
  • Pavement ants: Small, dark brown with striations on head and thorax. Nest in and under pavement, foundation slabs, and driveways. Common spring swarmers in basements and first floors of row homes.
  • Trail location and bait response help distinguish ant species and determine the right plan.
Kitchens & bathroomsMoisture sources

Look-Alike Guide — What You Saw vs. What You Think You Saw

The 10 Most Confused Pest Pairs in the Philadelphia Region

Some insects are genuinely dangerous; their look-alikes are not. Knowing the difference before panicking — or before over-treating — matters for homeowners, restaurants, and property managers.

Send a photo and tell Elite where the bug was found. That single step resolves 80% of the "what is this?" questions quickly.

Bed Bugs vs. Carpet Beetles

Bed bugs: flat, oval, reddish-brown, found in sleeping areas. Carpet beetles: round, patterned (black, white, orange), often found near windows, fabric, stored items, and baseboards. Carpet beetle larvae look like tiny, hairy worms — common near vents and under furniture. Carpet beetles do not bite blood.

Termite Swarmers vs. Flying Ants

Termite: equal-length wings, straight antennae, thick waist (no pinch). Flying ant: two wing sizes, bent antennae, pinched waist. Both emerge in spring, often near windows and doors. Save a sample on tape before cleanup — it is the most helpful thing you can do.

German Roaches vs. Wood Roaches

German roach: indoor pest, reproduces rapidly, mostly found in kitchen and bathroom. Wood roach: outdoor insect that wanders in near wooded lots, does not reproduce indoors, not a sign of infestation. A single roach near a door or window after heavy rain is often a wood roach.

Fleas vs. Springtails

Fleas jump and bite; springtails jump but do not bite. Springtails are found near moisture — in potted plant soil, bathroom tiles, damp basements, and concrete floors. They are harmless. Fleas are associated with pets, wildlife trails, or previous animal occupants.

Clover Mites vs. Bed Bugs or Chiggers

Clover mites are tiny red mites found on sunny windowsills, exterior walls, and on plants. They leave a red smear when crushed. They do not bite humans. Chiggers are outdoor arachnids found in tall grass and cause intense itching. Neither is a bed bug.

Centipedes vs. Millipedes

Centipedes: flat, fast, one pair of legs per segment, can bite if handled. Millipedes: rounded, slow, two pairs of legs per segment, curl up when disturbed. Both indicate moisture. Centipedes are predators; millipedes feed on decaying organic matter. Neither is dangerous in small numbers.

Wasps vs. Yellow Jackets vs. Hornets

Paper wasps: long, thin waist, open paper comb nest, less aggressive. Yellow jackets: stocky, yellow and black, nest in ground or wall voids, very aggressive. Bald-faced hornets: large, black and white, paper-covered football nest. All three are painful stingers, but treatment and approach differ by species.

Drain Flies vs. Fruit Flies vs. Fungus Gnats

Drain flies: fuzzy, moth-like, rest on walls near drains. Fruit flies: smooth, red-eyed, hover near overripe produce and bar spills. Fungus gnats: thin, dark, fly erratically near wet potting soil and houseplants. All three can indicate organic material buildup; source identification is key.

Pharaoh Ants vs. Odorous House Ants

Pharaoh ants: tiny, pale yellow, common in hospitals, large apartment buildings, and restaurant spaces. Multi-queen colonies that fragment under pressure — do not use over-the-counter repellent sprays, which cause colonies to bud. Odorous house ants: darker, coconut smell when crushed, common in homes. Different treatment strategies apply.

Stink Bugs vs. Shield Bugs vs. Squash Bugs

Brown marmorated stink bug: mottled brown, shield-shaped, an invasive species from Asia that congregates on home exteriors in fall. Native shield bugs: similar shape, usually greener or differently patterned, not an overwintering invader. Squash bug: darker, found on garden plants, not a home invader.

Seasonal Pest Calendar — Greater Philadelphia Region

What to Expect by Season in PA, NJ, and DE

Pest pressure in the Philadelphia tri-state area follows predictable seasonal patterns. Knowing what to expect each season helps with early prevention and faster service decisions.

These are general patterns. Warm winters, wet springs, and dry summers can shift timing significantly. If something is happening outside the expected window, it is still worth documenting and submitting a request.

Spring

March – May

  • Termite swarmers emerge
  • Ant colonies become active (pavement, carpenter, odorous house)
  • Yellow jacket queens start new nests
  • Stink bugs and boxelder bugs emerge from overwintering
  • Mosquito season begins in late April/May
  • Tick activity resumes above 35°F
  • Rodents increase foraging as nest food stores deplete
Summer

June – August

  • Mosquito season peaks (June–August)
  • Yellow jacket and hornet colonies reach full size
  • Flea season peaks — pets and yard activity
  • Deer tick activity continues through heat
  • Carpenter ant swarmers in late spring to early summer
  • Fruit flies and drain flies in restaurant and bar settings
  • Pantry pests active in dry storage areas
Fall

September – November

  • Stink bug invasion begins (September–October)
  • Boxelder bugs cluster on south-facing walls
  • Yellow jacket aggression peaks (September)
  • Rodents seek warmth indoors as temps drop
  • Centipedes move deeper indoors
  • Overwintering pest populations enter structures
  • Mosquito season winds down after first frost
Winter

December – February

  • Mouse and rat activity increases indoors
  • Stink bugs and overwintering pests dormant in wall voids
  • Silverfish and cockroaches active year-round indoors
  • Drain flies and pantry pests active in heated spaces
  • Warm spells can trigger brief wasp and ant activity
  • Deer ticks remain active above 35°F

How to Document a Pest Before Submitting a Request

A better photo and a clear location note turns a vague "what is this?" into a faster, more accurate service conversation.

You do not need a perfect photo. Location, size reference, and where the pest was found are often more useful than the image itself.

Add a Size Reference

Put a coin, pen tip, or ruler next to the bug if it is safe. Size is one of the most important identification clues.

Note the Location

Bed, mattress seam, kitchen, bathroom, windowsill, basement, attic, yard, drain, restaurant prep area, or commercial storage.

Track Repeat Sightings

Is this one bug, or are you seeing repeat activity? Daily, weekly, or tied to weather events? That pattern matters more than the bug alone.

Submit with Context

Use the service request form and include the property type, whether it is a business or rental, and any urgency details like an event or health inspection.
